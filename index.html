5. What risks impact digital projects in a negative way? What causes digital projects to fail and why?

A large percentage of digital projects fail, infact around 80% of digital transformation projects will be considered a failure. The disparity between expectations and the end result is a major reason why digital projects are not seen as a successful outcome. Some of the main reasons for this high percntage of failure can be;
Planning - A well developed plan should recognize a realistic time and budget for completion, allow for changes during product development, identify the recources and skills needed for success and also include risk mitigation. When a plan looks at possible risks and contingency planning, the probability of failure is reduced and the potential for success increased. Planning for after lunch is vital and should not be left until the last minute. Planning helps to bring clarity and ensures that everybody involved is on the same page.
Human Resource - Unclear project requirements - Having the required expertise and staying connected and engaged with all people involved. A strong experiences team, of the correct number of members (as in, not too little or too many) is crucial. A misconseption is that a digital project requires digital employees, when in fact people with expertise in business process and business strategy are just as important. Furthermore, anybody involved in signing off the project should be consistantly kept in the development loop and updated regularly. Leadership is crucial. Sucessful projects need good leaders with great management skills. Doing so can increase chances of sucessful and desirable outcome as any unrealistic expectations, along with product changes can be dealt with immediatly if communication is fluid. Being open and practicing fluid communication with everybody involved can staying connected to the clients or the rest of the team.
Business Process - All to often the hype of new technology overshadows business process implications. Projects are started and developed without being alighned with core business goals and digital strategy. New and innovative digital products become successful when they are built to be integrated into business operations and contribute towards the overall business objective. Being realistic with expetation and being prepared to work on the product after launch is also important, sometimes everything cannot be featured in the first release. Expectations must be equal to reality.
Amongst other risk factors, the expectation of the end product should be clearly understood by all involved in the project. The last thing you want is to create a product which does not live up to expectations.[5]

8. Legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects. How GDPR impacts digital projects?

The general Data Protection Regulations or (GDPR) came into effect 25th May 2018 and is the biggest data law overhaul in the last two decades. The new privacy landscape which is designed to protect EU citizens, applies to every business, charity and not-for-profit organisations. The regulations applies to all organisations that collect, store and use data and is an extension of the old 95 directive highlighting the nessecity for transparency, disclosure and process. The new framework takes today's technological advancements into account which was not an issue in previous years. It is as much about ethics as it is about legality and gives the user more control over how their data is collected, stored and used. The new law gives users the right to be informed, right of access, right to rectification, right to be forgotten(right to data deletion), right to restrict processing, right to data portability, right to object and rights in relation to automated decision making and profiling. Great news for users! For businesses and developers, compliance means developing software that is GDPR compliant and in line with the Design with privacy framework. Data such as names, adresses, IP and Mac addresses, email, phone numbers, location data and cookies are included under the term 'personal information'. No longer can software be designed to extract, store and use personal information without consent. That is no longer an option. GDPR means adding privacy by design and data protection by default to your testing processes, which should supplement existing procedures such as penetration testing. Going forward, developers will be required to: Programme with transparancy; in the ways data is collected and used; Improve consent mechanisms and user controls; Execute projects and applications which provide optimal control over consent settings. The Privacy by Design framework is a seven-point development methodology which requires optimal data protection to be provided as standard, by default, across all uses and applications. [8]
